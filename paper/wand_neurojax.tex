\begin{abstract}
Whole-brain computational models of neural dynamics require structural
connectivity, conduction delays, metabolic context, and electrophysiological
recordings as independent constraints. Existing studies typically lack one
or more of these measurements, forcing free-parameter assumptions that
limit predictive validity. The Welsh Advanced Neuroimaging Database
(WAND)~\citep{Mayerand2025wand} provides all of them in 170 healthy
volunteers: ultra-strong-gradient diffusion MRI (300~mT/m AxCaliber),
quantitative MRI (T1/T2/QMT/MP2RAGE), perfusion (pCASL, TRUST),
spectroscopy (GABA/glutamate), 275-channel MEG, and TMS-EEG.

We introduce \textbf{NeuroJAX}, a differentiable, GPU-accelerated Python
toolkit built on JAX~\citep{Bradbury2018jax} and
Equinox~\citep{Kidger2021equinox} that unifies preprocessing and
biophysical modelling in a single computational graph. NeuroJAX enables
end-to-end gradient descent from sensor-level error through hemodynamic
forward models to per-region biophysical parameters.

This paper presents the quantitative structural processing pipeline for
the reference WAND subject (sub-08033) and the first set of derived maps,
demonstrating that every free parameter in a standard neurovascular
coupling model can be independently measured or tightly constrained.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================================

Large-scale computational models of brain dynamics
\citep{Deco2013rww,Riera2006neurovascular} depend on three categories of
subject-specific input: (1) structural connectivity and conduction delays
from diffusion MRI, (2) regional tissue properties---myelination, metabolite
concentrations, perfusion---that constrain biophysical parameters, and
(3) functional recordings (MEG, fMRI, TMS-EEG) that provide the fitting
targets. The tension in the field is that most datasets provide only a
subset: the Human Connectome Project gives excellent diffusion and fMRI but
no TMS; clinical TMS-EEG studies rarely include microstructural MRI.

WAND~\citep{Mayerand2025wand} closes this gap with seven imaging sessions
per subject on the Connectom 300~mT/m scanner at CUBRIC Cardiff,
covering every modality needed for a fully-constrained neurovascular model.

\subsection{Differentiable generative modelling with the JAX ecosystem}

\paragraph{Generative models in neuroimaging.}
The generative modelling approach to neuroimaging inverts a forward
(biophysical) model to infer latent parameters from observed data.
Rather than treating brain images as static maps, generative models
simulate the physical processes that produce the measurements ---
neural mass dynamics, hemodynamic coupling, electromagnetic forward
projection, MR signal formation --- and fit the model parameters to
match empirical recordings. This approach has driven two decades of
progress in dynamic causal modelling (DCM), whole-brain network
simulation via The Virtual Brain
(TVB)~\citep{SanzLeon2013tvb}, and more recently
WhoBPyT~\citep{Griffiths2024whobpyt}, which recasts whole-brain
simulation as a deep network in PyTorch for gradient-based fitting.
The limitation of these tools is that they treat preprocessing and
modelling as separate stages, and their fitting procedures (Euler
integration, finite-difference gradients, grid search) do not scale
to the parameter dimensionality that multi-modal constraints demand.

\paragraph{Simulation-based inference and neural differential equations.}
Two developments in machine learning transform what is computationally
feasible. First, \textbf{simulation-based inference}
(SBI)~\citep{Cranmer2020sbi} replaces likelihood evaluation with
learned neural density estimators trained on simulated data, enabling
Bayesian posterior estimation for models with intractable likelihoods
--- exactly the situation in whole-brain simulation where the
mapping from parameters to observables passes through stiff ODEs and
nonlinear forward models. When coupled with GPU-accelerated
simulators, SBI makes it practical to explore parameter spaces of
hundreds of dimensions that were previously inaccessible. Second,
\textbf{neural ordinary differential equations}
(Neural ODEs)~\citep{Chen2018neuralode} and their extensions to
controlled and stochastic differential equations
(CDEs, SDEs)~\citep{Kidger2022neuralDE} embed neural networks within
differential equation solvers, enabling learned dynamics that respect
physical structure while remaining fully differentiable. The parallel
development of \textbf{physics-informed neural networks}
(PINNs)~\citep{Raissi2019pinns} --- which enforce PDE constraints as
soft penalties in the loss function --- provides a meshless alternative
to FEM for field problems (conductivity, elasticity, electromagnetic
forward models). Together, these methods enable a new class of
brain model: one where the tissue-level physics, the network dynamics,
and the observation forward models are all differentiable, composable,
and GPU-accelerated.

\paragraph{The JAX + Equinox + Diffrax stack.}
NeuroJAX is built on JAX~\citep{Bradbury2018jax} with
Equinox~\citep{Kidger2021equinox} for neural network modules and
Diffrax for differential equation solvers --- the same stack developed
by Kidger for Neural CDEs. This choice is deliberate: JAX provides
\texttt{jax.grad} (automatic differentiation through arbitrary Python),
\texttt{jax.vmap} (automatic vectorisation for batch processing),
\texttt{jax.jit} (XLA compilation to GPU/TPU), and
\texttt{jax.lax.scan} (efficient sequential computation). Equinox
provides PyTree-based neural network modules that compose naturally
with JAX transformations. Diffrax provides adaptive-step ODE/SDE/CDE
solvers with adjoint backpropagation. The result is that the entire
forward model --- from Bloch equation tissue simulation through
Balloon--Windkessel hemodynamics to sensor-level prediction --- compiles
into a single XLA computation graph through which gradients flow
end-to-end:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \sum_m w_m \, d_m\!\bigl(
    \mathbf{y}_m^{\text{sim}}(\theta;\, \mathbf{C},\mathbf{D},\mathbf{L}),\;
    \mathbf{y}_m^{\text{emp}}
  \bigr)
\end{equation}
%
where $\theta$ are biophysical parameters, $\mathbf{C}$/$\mathbf{D}$ are
the structural connectome weights/delays, $\mathbf{L}$ is the leadfield,
and $m$ indexes modalities (FC, FCD, TEP waveform, sensor MSE). This
enables gradient-based fitting with per-region heterogeneous parameters
across hundreds of brain regions --- a problem that grid search or
finite-difference methods cannot touch, but that
\texttt{jax.grad} solves in seconds on a GPU.

\section{Dataset}

\subsection{CUBRIC and the Connectom scanner}

The Cardiff University Brain Research Imaging Centre (CUBRIC) has been
at the forefront of human connectomics since the installation of a
Siemens MAGNETOM Connectom~3T scanner with 300\,mT/m gradients in
2016 --- the first in Europe and only the second worldwide after the
Massachusetts General Hospital system built for the Human Connectome
Project~\citep{Jones2022connectom}. Under the direction of
Derek~K.~Jones, CUBRIC has used these ultra-strong gradients to map
white matter microstructure at unprecedented precision, enabling
AxCaliber axon diameter estimation and CHARMED restricted-fraction
imaging in~vivo.

Complementing the diffusion programme, Krish~D.~Singh's group has
pioneered the combination of magnetic resonance spectroscopy with
electrophysiology~\citep{Muthukumaraswamy2009gaba}, demonstrating
that resting GABA concentration in visual cortex predicts peak gamma
oscillation frequency and fMRI BOLD amplitude. This established a
direct, measurable link between inhibitory neurotransmitter
concentration and neural dynamics --- a founding principle of the WAND
dataset design and the MRS-constrained neural mass modelling in this
work. The Singh group's subsequent studies extended this to
glutamate, schizophrenia risk markers, and motor cortex, building
the empirical case that MRS metabolites can serve as independent
constraints on computational models of cortical circuit function.

WAND~\citep{Mayerand2025wand} synthesises these programmes into a
single cohort of 170 healthy volunteers (aged 18--63, BIDS-organised),
acquired across up to 8 sessions on three scanner platforms at CUBRIC.
The dataset was published in \textit{Scientific Data} (2024) and is
available via GIN (\url{https://gin.g-node.org/CUBRIC/WAND}). To our
knowledge, no computational modelling studies have yet been published
using WAND; the present work represents the first application of
differentiable biophysical modelling to this resource.

\subsection{Scanner platforms and sessions}

The WAND sessions span four distinct instrument platforms:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Siemens MAGNETOM Connectom 3T} (300\,mT/m, ses-02,
    ses-06): Ultra-strong gradients for diffusion microstructure
    (AxCaliber, CHARMED) and quantitative relaxometry (VFA, QMT).
    Sub-mm structural imaging (MP2RAGE 0.7\,mm, 7-echo GRE 0.67\,mm).
  \item \textbf{Siemens MAGNETOM Prisma 3T} (80\,mT/m, 32-channel
    receive-only head coil, ses-03, ses-05): fMRI, perfusion (pCASL,
    TRUST), structural (T1w, T2w), and MRS (semi-LASER).
  \item \textbf{Siemens MAGNETOM 7T} (32-channel receive / volume
    transmit head coil, Nova Medical, ses-04): High-field MRS
    (semi-LASER) for improved spectral resolution of GABA and
    glutamate.
  \item \textbf{CTF 275-channel radial gradiometer MEG} (ses-01):
    Sampled at 1200\,Hz, analysed as synthetic third-order
    gradiometers with 29 reference channels. EOG recorded
    concurrently. Five paradigms: resting-state, auditory-motor,
    mismatch negativity, Simon task, visual.
  \item \textbf{Magstim BiStim\textsuperscript{2} TMS} (ses-08,
    Magstim, Whitland, UK): D70 Alpha Flat figure-of-eight coil
    (70\,mm). Neuronavigated with BrainSight (Rogue Research,
    Montreal) and Polaris optical tracking (Northern Digital).
    Controlled via CED~1401 interface with Signal~v6.04a software.
    EMG recorded with Digitimer D440-4 amplifier and pre-gelled
    Ag/AgCl electrodes. SICI paired-pulse protocol, 3 runs per
    subject.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{CUBRIC-provided derivatives}

In addition to raw BIDS data, CUBRIC distributes several pre-computed
derivatives:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Brain-extracted T1w images} via HD-BET for each session
  \item \textbf{Diffusion QC}: FSL \texttt{eddy\_qc} reports (motion
    parameters, outlier counts, CNR maps) for the multishell DWI
  \item \textbf{Structural and functional QC}: MRIQC reports for T1w,
    T2w, and BOLD data
  \item \textbf{MEG--MRI coregistrations}: T1w images transformed to
    CTF MEG coordinate space (Cardiff GitLab)
  \item \textbf{Phenotype data}: age, sex, ethnicity, anthropometrics
    (weight, height, waist/hip, blood pressure), alcohol and smoking
    status (\texttt{participants.tsv})
  \item \textbf{Cognitive and behavioural data}: trait questionnaires
    and task performance (\texttt{phenotype/} directory)
\end{itemize}

\noindent The processing reported here extends these CUBRIC derivatives
in three directions: (1)~quantitative tissue property maps from the
rich relaxometry acquisitions (VFA~T1, QMT~BPF, T2*, g-ratio,
MP2RAGE~T1); (2)~advanced subcortical segmentations (DTI-enhanced
thalamic nuclei, hippocampal subfields, hypothalamic subunits);
and (3)~perfusion quantification (CBF, OEF, CMRO$_2$). We also
extend the diffusion and microstructural derivatives beyond CUBRIC's
eddy QC reports with DTI fitting (FA, MD, V1 from the CHARMED
low-$b$ shells), and lay the groundwork for AxCaliber axon diameter
estimation and microstructure-informed connectome construction via
sbi4dwi on the full ultra-strong-gradient data.

\section{Methods}
% ============================================================================

The breadth of the WAND dataset demands a correspondingly broad
processing toolkit. Two complementary open-source projects ---
FreeSurfer and FSL --- form the backbone of our pipeline, together
covering every modality in the dataset.

\textbf{FreeSurfer}~\citep{Fischl2012freesurfer} grew out of the
cortical surface reconstruction work of Anders Dale and Martin Sereno
at UC San Diego in the early
1990s~\citep{Dale1999cortical,Fischl1999cortical}. Bruce Fischl,
joining as a postdoctoral fellow, replaced the manual steps of the
surface pipeline with automated algorithms; the collaboration
continued as Fischl and Douglas Greve built FreeSurfer into a
production tool at the Athinoula A.\ Martinos Center for Biomedical
Imaging, Massachusetts General Hospital, where it has been developed
and maintained ever since. Over two decades it has grown into the
standard tool for cortical parcellation, subcortical segmentation,
and surface-based analysis. Recent versions (8.x) add
deep-learning modules --- SynthSeg for contrast-invariant
segmentation~\citep{Billot2023synthseg}, SynthMorph for
contrast-agnostic registration~\citep{Hoffmann2022synthmorph}, and
CNN-based thalamic and hippocampal segmentation --- that extend its
reach to the heterogeneous multi-contrast data in WAND.

\textbf{FSL} (FMRIB Software Library)~\citep{Jenkinson2012fsl,Smith2004fsl}
was first released in 2000 from the Oxford Centre for Functional MRI of the
Brain (FMRIB) and has become the most widely used neuroimaging analysis
suite, with tools spanning structural (FAST, FIRST, fsl\_anat), diffusion
(eddy, bedpostx, probtrackx2, xtract, dtifit), functional (FEAT, MELODIC,
FIX), and registration (FLIRT, FNIRT, MMORF). Critically for WAND, FSL
has extended beyond its MRI core into two additional domains:
\textbf{perfusion imaging}, via oxford\_asl and
BASIL/FABBER~\citep{Chappell2009basil} for ASL quantification, and
\textbf{magnetic resonance spectroscopy}, via
FSL-MRS~\citep{Clarke2021fslmrs} for end-to-end SVS and MRSI analysis.
Through the Oxford Centre for Human Brain Activity (OHBA), the FSL
ecosystem further extends to \textbf{electrophysiology}: osl-ephys
provides MEG/EEG preprocessing and source
reconstruction~\citep{vanEs2025oslephys}, while osl-dynamics implements
HMM and DyNeMo brain state models~\citep{Gohil2023dynemo}. This
remarkable convergence means that a single software ecosystem ---
FSL~+~OHBA --- can process structural MRI, diffusion, fMRI, perfusion,
spectroscopy, and MEG from WAND, with FreeSurfer providing the
complementary cortical surface infrastructure and a host of tools for
advanced structural processing.

NeuroJAX and vpjax add the differentiable modelling layer that these
preprocessing tools lack: end-to-end gradient-based fitting from
tissue-level biophysics through hemodynamics to sensor-level
predictions, all compiled in JAX for GPU acceleration.

\subsection{FreeSurfer recon-all + advanced segmentations}

Standard cortical reconstruction was performed with FreeSurfer~8.2.0
(\texttt{recon-all} with T2-weighted pial refinement from ses-03).
Additional segmentations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{SAMSEG}~\citep{Puonti2016samseg}: joint T1w+T2w Bayesian
        segmentation, providing tissue probability maps and intracranial volume.
  \item \textbf{SynthSeg}~\citep{Billot2023synthseg}: contrast-invariant DL
        segmentation applied to T1w from ses-02, 03, 04, 05 for cross-session
        volumetric consistency.
  \item \textbf{Hypothalamic subunits}: DL segmentation (Billot et al.\ 2020).
  \item \textbf{Thalamic nuclei}: FreeSurfer atlas-based segmentation with
        optional DTI enhancement.
  \item \textbf{Hippocampal subfields + amygdala nuclei}: with T2w for
        improved subfield boundaries.
\end{itemize}

  \frac{S}{\sin\alpha} = E_1 \frac{S}{\tan\alpha} + M_0(1-E_1),
  \qquad E_1 = e^{-\text{TR}/T_1}
\end{equation}

\subsection{QMT bound pool fraction}

Quantitative magnetization transfer was fitted from 11 MT-on volumes
(3 MT flip angles $\times$ multiple offset frequencies: 1--56\,kHz) plus
one MT-off reference, using a two-pool Ramani model~\citep{Ramani2002qmt}
with super-Lorentzian lineshape for the bound pool. The bound pool
fraction (BPF) provides a direct measure of macromolecular (myelin)
content~\citep{Sled2001qmt}.

(from the phase data, not yet processed), iron and myelin contributions
can be separated.

\subsection{Quantitative perfusion processing}

The full perfusion pipeline combines three independent measurements
--- cerebral blood flow, venous oxygenation, and blood T1 --- to yield
the cerebral metabolic rate of oxygen consumption (CMRO$_2$). CBF is
quantified with FSL's \texttt{oxford\_asl}, which wraps the BASIL
(Bayesian Inference for Arterial Spin Labelling) framework
\citep{Alsop2015asl}. BASIL itself uses FABBER, FSL's variational
Bayesian inference engine, to invert the kinetic model with spatial
regularisation and partial-volume correction. OEF is obtained from
TRUST, and subject-specific blood T1 from the Look-Locker inversion
recovery acquisition. These combine via Fick's principle to give
CMRO$_2$.

\subsubsection{CBF from pCASL}

Cerebral blood flow was quantified from pseudo-continuous ASL
(bolus~=~1.8\,s, PLD~=~2.0\,s, 110 volumes) using FSL
\texttt{oxford\_asl}~\citep{Alsop2015asl} with voxelwise M0 calibration,
motion correction, and partial-volume correction. The ASL white paper
single-compartment model~\citep{Alsop2015asl} gives:
%
\begin{equation}
  \text{CBF} = \frac{6000 \cdot \lambda \cdot (\overline{S}_{\text{ctrl}} - \overline{S}_{\text{label}})
    \cdot e^{\text{PLD}/T_{1b}}}
    {2 \cdot \alpha \cdot T_{1b} \cdot M_0 \cdot (1 - e^{-\tau/T_{1b}})}
    \quad [\text{ml}/100\text{g}/\text{min}]
\end{equation}
%
where $\lambda$~=~0.9\,ml/g is the blood--brain partition coefficient,
$\alpha$~=~0.85 the labelling efficiency, $\tau$~=~1.8\,s the bolus
duration, and $T_{1b}$ the subject-specific blood T1.

\paragraph{Blood T1 from inversion recovery.}
Subject-specific $T_{1b}$ was estimated from the Look-Locker acquisition
(128$\times$128$\times$1, 960 time points, TR~=~150\,ms). The signal
in a sagittal sinus ROI was fitted to:
%
\begin{equation}
  S(t) = A\bigl(1 - B\,e^{-t/T_1^*}\bigr),
  \qquad T_1 = T_1^* \cdot B
\end{equation}
%
where the Deichmann--Haase correction recovers true $T_1$ from the
apparent $T_1^*$ and the inversion efficiency $B$.

\subsubsection{TRUST: venous oxygenation}

T2 of venous blood in the sagittal sinus was estimated from the
TRUST acquisition~\citep{Lu2008trust} (24 volumes, 4 effective TEs
$\times$ 3 repeats $\times$ control/label). The control--label
difference signal in the sinus decays as:
%
\begin{equation}
  |\Delta S|(\text{eTE}) = S_0 \, e^{-\text{eTE}/T_2}
\end{equation}
%
Venous T2 is calibrated to oxygen saturation $Y$ via the
quadratic model~\citep{Lu2012bloodt2} at 3\,T:
%
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1}{T_2} = A + B(1-Y) + C(1-Y)^2
  \label{eq:t2y}
\end{equation}
%
with coefficients $A$~=~4.5, $B$~=~47.1, $C$~=~55.5\,s$^{-1}$
for Hct~=~0.42. Solving the quadratic for $Y$ gives SvO$_2$,
from which OEF follows.

\subsubsection{CMRO$_2$ via Fick's principle}

The three independent perfusion measurements combine via the
Fick equation to give the cerebral metabolic rate of oxygen consumption:
%
\begin{equation}
  \text{CMRO}_2 = \text{CBF} \times \text{OEF} \times C_aO_2
\end{equation}
%
where OEF~=~(SaO$_2$~$-$~SvO$_2$)/SaO$_2$ from TRUST and the
arterial oxygen content is:
%
\begin{equation}
  C_aO_2 = [\text{Hb}] \times 1.34 \times \text{SaO}_2
    \approx 8.8\;\mu\text{mol\,O}_2/\text{ml\,blood}
\end{equation}
%
for Hb~=~15\,g/dl and SaO$_2$~=~0.98. This hierarchy from global OEF
(TRUST) can be upgraded to regional OEF via quantitative
BOLD~\citep{He2007qbold} fitting of the 7-echo GRE (ses-06).

\subsection{Diffusion microstructure processing}

Diffusion-weighted images from ses-02 (AxCaliber: 4 shells,
$b$~=~0--15\,500\,s/mm$^2$; CHARMED: AP+PA) were preprocessed with
FSL's standard distortion and motion correction pipeline.
\texttt{topup} estimated the susceptibility-induced off-resonance
field from the AP/PA $b$=0 image pair, and \texttt{eddy} corrected
for eddy-current distortions, subject motion, and signal dropout
(outlier replacement enabled). CUBRIC-provided \texttt{eddy\_qc}
reports confirmed low motion and few outlier slices for sub-08033.

DTI fitting was performed with \texttt{dtifit} on the low-$b$ shells
($b \leq 2000$\,s/mm$^2$), yielding fractional anisotropy (FA), mean
diffusivity (MD), and the principal eigenvector (V1). Multi-fibre
estimation used \texttt{bedpostx} (Bayesian Estimation of Diffusion
Parameters Obtained using Sampling Techniques), modelling up to 3
crossing fibres per voxel with the \texttt{zeppelin} model for the
extra-axonal compartment.

Tract segmentation employed \texttt{xtract}, FSL's automated
tractography tool, which segments 42 major white-matter tracts using
standardised seed/target/exclusion ROIs in MNI space, warped to
native diffusion space via nonlinear registration
(\texttt{fnirt}). Probabilistic tractography was run with
\texttt{probtrackx2} using the bedpostx fibre orientations with
5000 streamlines per seed voxel.

Whole-brain structural connectomes ($\mathbf{C}$, $\mathbf{L}$) were
constructed by seeding streamlines from each parcel of the
Desikan--Killiany cortical atlas (from FreeSurfer) and counting
streamline endpoints in every other parcel. The connection weight
matrix $\mathbf{C}$ stores streamline counts (log-normalised) and the
fibre length matrix $\mathbf{L}$ stores median streamline lengths.
Combined with g-ratio from QMT BPF and the Hursh--Rushton relationship,
$\mathbf{L}$ yields the conduction delay matrix
$\mathbf{D}_{ij} = L_{ij} / v_{ij}$ with per-connection velocity
$v_{ij}$.

For microstructural modelling beyond DTI, \texttt{sbi4dwi}
(simulation-based inference for diffusion MRI) will fit the AxCaliber
model to the full ultra-strong-gradient data, estimating axon diameter
distributions and restricted signal fractions. DIPY serves as a
complementary toolkit for signal reconstruction, tractography
algorithm comparison, and diffusion spectrum imaging.

\subsection{Resting-state fMRI processing}

WAND ses-03 includes resting-state BOLD fMRI on the Prisma 3T,
providing the functional connectivity targets against which structural
connectivity and simulated dynamics are compared.

\paragraph{Preprocessing.}
Resting fMRI preprocessing follows the FSL pipeline: motion correction
(\texttt{mcflirt}), brain extraction (\texttt{bet}), spatial smoothing,
high-pass temporal filtering, and registration to standard space via
the \texttt{fsl\_anat} structural output (FLIRT~+~FNIRT). Alternatively,
fMRIPrep~\citep{Esteban2019fmriprep} provides a BIDS-native
preprocessing workflow with automated confound regression, fieldmap
correction, and surface projection via FreeSurfer.

\paragraph{Independent component analysis.}
Resting-state networks are identified using FSL's
MELODIC~\citep{Beckmann2004melodic}, which performs probabilistic
independent component analysis (ICA) on the 4D BOLD data to decompose
it into spatially independent components with associated time courses.
Group-level ICA across WAND subjects will identify the canonical
resting-state networks that Smith et
al.~\citep{Smith2009correspondence} defined by demonstrating a
one-to-one correspondence between BrainMap task-activation networks
and resting-state networks derived from ICA of nearly 30,000 subjects.
These canonical networks --- including default mode, dorsal attention,
salience, visual, sensorimotor, frontoparietal, and language --- serve
as the functional parcellation against which whole-brain model
predictions of functional connectivity (FC) and functional connectivity
dynamics (FCD) are evaluated.

\paragraph{Artefact removal.}
FSL's FIX (FMRIB's ICA-based X-noisifier) provides automated
classification of ICA components into signal and noise (motion,
physiological, scanner artefacts), enabling data-driven denoising
without the need for explicit confound regressors. For WAND, where
physiological monitoring data are partially available, FIX offers a
robust alternative to nuisance regression.

\paragraph{Dual regression and network modelling.}
Subject-specific network maps are obtained via dual regression against
the group ICA templates, yielding per-subject spatial maps and time
courses for each canonical network. These time courses provide the
empirical FC and FCD matrices that serve as fitting targets for the
NeuroJAX whole-brain model (Eq.~1).
Bijsterbosch, Smith, and Beckmann~\citep{Bijsterbosch2017rsfmri}
provide a comprehensive treatment of these methods in their Oxford
Neuroimaging Primer on resting-state fMRI functional connectivity.

\subsection{MEG processing}

MEG data from ses-01 (CTF 275-channel, 1200\,Hz, synthetic
third-order gradiometers) were processed using the
\texttt{osl-ephys} pipeline. Pre-processing comprised:
(i)~downsampling to 250\,Hz, (ii)~bandpass filtering (1--45\,Hz for
resting-state, broadband for task), (iii)~automated bad-channel
detection and interpolation, and (iv)~ICA artifact rejection via
\texttt{osl\_dynamics.preprocessing}, removing ocular (EOG-correlated)
and cardiac components by temporal correlation and topographic
template matching.

Source reconstruction used the RHINO (Registration of Headshapes
Including Nose in OSL) coregistration procedure, which aligns the
Polaris-digitised headshape to the FreeSurfer scalp surface
extracted from the ses-03 T1w. A single-shell boundary element model
(BEM) provided the forward model for beamforming (linearly
constrained minimum variance, LCMV) and minimum-norm estimation.
Source time courses were extracted at each parcel centroid of the
Desikan--Killiany atlas and sign-flipped to resolve the inherent
source orientation ambiguity across subjects.

For dynamic brain state analysis, parcellated source envelopes were
prepared with time-delay embedding (TDE, $\pm$7 lags) and PCA
dimensionality reduction (retaining 95\% variance). This
spatio-temporal feature space served as input to
\texttt{osl-dynamics}, which provides both the Hidden Markov Model
(HMM) of \citet{Vidaurre2018hmm} and DyNeMo
\citep{Gohil2023dynemo}, a deep generative model that estimates
time-varying linear mixtures of mode covariances. State/mode spectra,
fractional occupancies, lifetimes, and switching rates provide the
functional summary statistics against which the NeuroJAX whole-brain
simulation is validated.

\subsection{MRS processing}

Magnetic resonance spectroscopy data were acquired with the
semi-LASER (sLASER) sequence at both 3\,T (ses-05, Prisma) and
7\,T (ses-04). Spectra were processed with \texttt{fsl\_mrs}
\citep{Clarke2021fslmrs}, FSL's spectroscopy quantification tool,
which performs frequency-and-phase alignment, coil combination,
eddy-current correction, water reference scaling, and LCModel-style
basis set fitting in the frequency domain.

Voxel localisation used \texttt{svs\_segment}: the MRS voxel was
registered to the ses-03 T1w and tissue fractions (GM, WM, CSF) were
computed from FAST segmentation within the voxel bounding box. These
fractions enable water-scaled absolute quantification of metabolite
concentrations (institutional units, i.u.) and partial-volume
correction for tissue-specific concentrations.

The primary metabolites of interest are GABA and glutamate (Glu).
Following the approach of \citet{Muthukumaraswamy2009gaba}, regional
GABA concentration constrains the inhibitory synaptic coupling
$J_I \propto [\text{GABA}]$ in the Reduced Wong-Wang neural mass
model~\citep{Deco2013rww}, while glutamate constrains the excitatory
NMDA coupling $J_\text{NMDA} \propto [\text{Glu}]$. With WAND's
four voxel placements (ACC, occipital, auditory, sensorimotor), these
constraints cover both default-mode and primary sensory regions,
reducing parameter degeneracy in the whole-brain fit. MEGA-PRESS
data from ses-05 provide an additional GABA estimate for
test--retest reliability assessment.

\subsection{TMS-EEG processing}

TMS data from ses-08 used the short-interval intracortical inhibition
(SICI) paired-pulse protocol with the Magstim BiStim$^2$ system.
SICI pairs a subthreshold conditioning stimulus (80\% RMT, 2\,ms ISI)
with a suprathreshold test stimulus to probe GABAergic inhibition in
motor cortex. Three runs were acquired per subject.

Analysis extracts TMS-evoked potentials (TEPs) from the
CED-recorded EEG/EMG data, time-locked to the TMS pulse.
After artifact rejection (TMS pulse artifact blanking, muscle
artifact ICA removal), the TEP waveform is epoched
($-$100 to +500\,ms) and baseline-corrected. Key TEP components
(N15, P30, N45, P60, N100, P180) index successive stages of cortical
processing: early components ($<$50\,ms) reflect local excitability
while late components ($>$100\,ms) reflect network propagation through
long-range connections.

The SICI ratio (conditioned MEP amplitude / unconditioned MEP
amplitude) provides a functional readout of GABAergic inhibition that
complements the MRS GABA concentration, linking neurochemistry to
cortical excitability. In the NeuroJAX framework, TEP waveforms serve
as fitting targets: the whole-brain model receives a simulated TMS
impulse at the stimulation site and the predicted network response is
compared to the measured TEP, enabling gradient-based optimisation of
connection-specific parameters.

\subsection{vpjax: Differentiable vascular physiology}

The quantitative structural maps feed into \textbf{vpjax} (Virtual
Physiology in JAX), the vascular/metabolic complement to vbjax
(Virtual Brain in JAX). Where vbjax provides neural mass models, vpjax
provides the complete physiological forward chain from neural activity
to measurable haemodynamic signals:
%
\begin{equation}
  \underbrace{z(t)}_{\text{neural}} \;\xrightarrow{\text{CMRO}_2}\;
  \underbrace{f(t)}_{\text{CBF}} \;\xrightarrow{\text{Balloon}}\;
  \underbrace{v(t),\; q(t)}_{\text{volume, deoxy-Hb}} \;\xrightarrow{T_2^*}\;
  \underbrace{y(t)}_{\text{BOLD/ASL/qBOLD}}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Balloon--Windkessel hemodynamics}

The extended Riera~\citep{Riera2006neurovascular} neurovascular model
replaces the simplified Buxton hemodynamics with a full metabolic
intermediate chain:
%
\begin{align}
  \dot{s} &= z - \kappa\, s - \gamma\,(f - 1) \label{eq:bw_s}\\
  \dot{f} &= s \label{eq:bw_f}\\
  \tau\,\dot{v} &= f - v^{1/\alpha} \label{eq:bw_v}\\
  \tau\,\dot{q} &= f\,\frac{E(f,\,\text{OEF}_0)}{E_0} - \frac{q}{v}\,v^{1/\alpha}
    \label{eq:bw_q}
\end{align}
%
where $E(f, E_0) = 1 - (1-E_0)^{1/f}$ is the oxygen extraction fraction
under the Grubb assumption, $\alpha$ is the Grubb exponent, $\kappa$ is the
signal decay rate, $\gamma$ the autoregulatory feedback, and $\tau$ the
transit time.

\subsubsection{qBOLD: regional OEF from multi-echo GRE}

The key upgrade from global OEF (TRUST) to regional OEF uses the
quantitative BOLD model~\citep{He2007qbold}:
%
\begin{equation}
  S(\text{TE}) = S_0 \;\cdot\; \mathcal{F}(\text{OEF},\,\text{DBV},\,\text{TE})
    \;\cdot\; e^{-R_2 \cdot \text{TE}}
  \label{eq:qbold}
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathcal{F}$ models the intravoxel frequency distribution from
randomly oriented deoxygenated vessels. At short TE the signal is
dominated by $R_2$ (tissue T2); at long TE by $R_2'$ (reversible,
from deoxy-Hb). The difference $R_2' = R_2^* - R_2 \propto
\text{OEF} \times \text{DBV} \times B_0$. WAND's 7-echo GRE
(TE~=~5--35\,ms) is ideal for fitting this model.

\subsubsection{Local linearization integration}

The Balloon--Windkessel equations (Eqs.~\ref{eq:bw_s}--\ref{eq:bw_q})
are stiff: the vascular response evolves on a $\sim$1\,s timescale while
neural dynamics operate at $\sim$1\,ms. Standard Euler or RK4 integration
requires impractically small time steps. vpjax uses the
\textbf{local linearization} (LL) method (Ozaki 1992, extended by Riera),
which analytically solves the linearised system at each step:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}(t+\Delta t) = e^{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{x}_t)\,\Delta t}\,\mathbf{x}(t)
    + \mathbf{J}^{-1}\!\bigl(e^{\mathbf{J}\,\Delta t} - \mathbf{I}\bigr)\,\mathbf{f}_0
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathbf{J} = \partial\mathbf{f}/\partial\mathbf{x}$ is the Jacobian
evaluated at the current state. This permits large time steps ($\Delta t$
up to 10$\times$ Euler) while remaining stable, and is fully differentiable
via \texttt{jax.grad} through the matrix exponential, enabling gradient-based
fitting of hemodynamic parameters alongside neural mass parameters in a
single optimization.

\subsubsection{Meshless physics: from tissue geometry to field solutions}

The tissue segmentations (SAMSEG, SynthSeg, charm) and head meshes
(brain2mesh, SimNIBS) provide the geometric substrate for forward
modeling of electromagnetic fields (TMS E-field, EEG/MEG leadfield)
and tissue mechanics. Traditional FEM approaches require mesh
generation, which is fragile, slow, and non-differentiable. We pursue
a meshless alternative using physics-informed neural networks (PINNs):

\paragraph{Conductivity estimation.}
The static current equation governs current flow through heterogeneous
tissue:
%
\begin{equation}
  \nabla \cdot \bigl(\sigma(\mathbf{x})\,\nabla V(\mathbf{x})\bigr) = -I_s(\mathbf{x})
  \label{eq:poisson}
\end{equation}
%
where $\sigma(\mathbf{x})$ is the tissue conductivity tensor and
$V(\mathbf{x})$ the electric potential. In sbi4dwi, both $V$ and
$\sigma$ are parameterised as Equinox MLPs and jointly optimised by
minimising the PDE residual (Eq.~\ref{eq:poisson}), electrode data
mismatch, and a pseudo-CT conductivity prior from WAND's quantitative
MRI:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{EIT} = \underbrace{\|\nabla\cdot(\sigma_\theta\nabla V_\phi)\|^2}_{\text{PDE}}
    + \lambda_d \underbrace{\|V_\phi(\mathbf{x}_e) - V_\text{meas}\|^2}_{\text{data}}
    + \lambda_r \underbrace{\|\sigma_\theta - \sigma_\text{prior}\|^2}_{\text{prior}}
\end{equation}

This meshless approach is fully differentiable via
\texttt{jax.grad}, enabling end-to-end backpropagation from
sensor-level TMS/EEG predictions through the conductivity model to
tissue property maps --- connecting the tissue segmentation pipeline
directly to the electromagnetic forward model without mesh generation.

\paragraph{Connection to Living Matter Physics.}
The same PINN framework extends to brain biomechanics
\citep{Kuhl2024livingmatter}: tissue elasticity, viscoelasticity, and
growth can be modelled by replacing the Poisson equation with the
equilibrium equations of continuum mechanics, using the same meshless
neural network solver. WAND's quantitative MRI (T1, T2*, QMT) provides
tissue-specific material property priors, and MR elastography (if
acquired) would provide direct validation. The differentiable nature of
JAX enables coupling mechanical deformation fields with neural dynamics
--- for example, predicting how traumatic brain injury alters
connectome-level conduction delays.

\subsection{neurojax.qmri: GPU-accelerated fitting}

The \texttt{neurojax.qmri} module provides differentiable signal models
in JAX: SPGR, bSSFP, IR-SPGR, multi-echo, QMT two-pool
(super-Lorentzian), and MP2RAGE. The \texttt{VoxelwiseFitter} engine
uses \texttt{jax.vmap} to fit all $\sim$1\,M voxels simultaneously on
GPU with analytic gradients from \texttt{jax.grad}, bounded parameter
optimisation via sigmoid reparameterisation, and Laplace uncertainty
from the Hessian.

\subsection{Cortical layer analysis with LAYNII}

The sub-mm ses-06 data (MEGRE 0.67\,mm, MP2RAGE 0.7\,mm) enables
cortical depth-resolved analysis using LAYNII~v2.10.0:
\texttt{LN2\_LAYERS} for equivolume depth definition,
\texttt{LN2\_PROFILE} for sampling R2*, QSM, T1, BPF per layer.
Deep layers (V--VI, feedforward, higher iron) vs.\ superficial
(I--III, feedback, heavier myelination) maps to the
$\xi$-$\alpha$NET hierarchy~\citep{ValdesSosa2026xialphanet}.

\section{Discussion}
% ============================================================================

The quantitative structural processing of the WAND reference subject
(sub-08033) is now largely complete: 17 processing pipelines across
5 sessions, producing over 80 derived maps and volumes from 4 independent
qMRI fitting tools (QUIT, qMRLab, FreeSurfer, neurojax). The key results
to date include:

\begin{itemize}
  \item QUIT Ramani QMT: BPF = 0.163 (WM), 0.082 (GM)
  \item Three-way T1 validation: WM = 650--727\,ms across three methods
  \item CBF from oxford\_asl: GM = 113, WM = 24\,ml/100g/min
  \item CMRO$_2$ = 109\,\textmu mol/100g/min (global OEF)
  \item 46 bilateral thalamic nuclei (DTI-enhanced CNN)
  \item Hippocampal subfields + amygdala nuclei
  \item Cross-session SynthSeg volumetrics (4 sessions)
\end{itemize}

\noindent With these structural foundations, we anticipate the following
from the remaining processing:
feasibility of extracting independent biophysical measurements for every
parameter in a neurovascular coupling model from a single dataset. With
the WAND data fully processed, we anticipate the following results:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Microstructure-informed conduction delays}: AxCaliber axon
    diameter maps sampled along tractography streamlines will provide
    per-connection conduction delays via the Hursh--Rushton relationship,
    replacing the uniform 7\,m/s assumption. Combined with the g-ratio
    from QMT BPF~\citep{Stikov2015gratio}, this gives connection-specific
    velocity matrices ($\mathbf{D}$).

  \item \textbf{MRS-constrained neural mass parameters}: GABA and glutamate
    concentrations from sLASER MRS (ses-04/05) will constrain the
    inhibitory ($J_I \propto [\text{GABA}]$) and excitatory
    ($J_\text{NMDA} \propto [\text{Glu}]$) coupling strengths in the
    Reduced Wong-Wang model~\citep{Deco2013rww}, reducing parameter
    degeneracy.

  \item \textbf{T1w/T2w myelin proxy validation}: Vertex-wise correlation
    of the T1w/T2w ratio~\citep{Glasser2011myelin} against QMT BPF
    (direct myelin measurement) will quantify the accuracy of the cheap
    proxy used in the $\xi$-$\alpha$NET model~\citep{ValdesSosa2026xialphanet}
    for constraining cortical hierarchy and conduction delays.

  \item \textbf{Regional CMRO$_2$ from qBOLD}: The 7-echo GRE enables
    quantitative BOLD fitting~\citep{He2007qbold} for per-voxel OEF,
    upgrading the global TRUST estimate to spatially resolved oxygen
    metabolism. Combined with pCASL CBF, this gives regional CMRO$_2$
    as a metabolic constraint on neural mass excitability.

  \item \textbf{TMS virtual lesion prediction}: After fitting the
    whole-brain model to TMS-evoked potentials (ses-08), selective
    connectome disconnection~\citep{Momi2023virtuallesion} will reveal
    which structural features predict the local$\to$network transition
    time --- the point ($\sim$100\,ms post-TMS) where network feedback
    exceeds local reverberation.

  \item \textbf{Cross-modal prediction}: Ridge regression with
    permutation-based feature importance will test which combination of
    structural (connectome topology), microstructural (axon diameter,
    g-ratio), functional (HMM state occupancies~\citep{Vidaurre2018hmm}),
    and metabolic (GABA, CBF) features best predicts individual TMS
    response amplitude and complexity.
\end{enumerate}

The key contribution is not any single analysis but the \emph{elimination
of free parameters}: where previous studies fit conduction velocity as a
free parameter, we measure it; where they assume uniform E/I balance, we
constrain it from spectroscopy. NeuroJAX provides the differentiable
computational infrastructure to propagate all of these measurements through
a unified biophysical model via \texttt{jax.grad}.
